Sweet  Egmont

This is the twelfth and last in a series of columns of historical interest about places on the Sunshine Coast. It’s meant to help visitors and newbies to get to know something about us, but it’s also useful to old-timers who’ve forgotten (or never learned) some of our stories.

Folks who’ve never been to tiny Egmont because they were in a rush to catch the Earl’s Cove ferry to Powell River missed the chance to explore one of the sweetest and most unique communities on the Coast. You have to turn off the highway and make a side trip of about 6 km to find it. In a recent  ”Voices of Egmont” column in this newspaper, Jackie Montgomery let us in on some goings on in this largely isolated  village. Folks gather here for movie night at the Community Centre, she reported, or they attend history lectures at the Egmont Heritage Centre, and they play in Texas Hold”Em tournaments at the Backeddy Pub. More fun than going home and watching TV, eh?

Egmont is located on the southwest side of the mouth of Sechelt Inlet, north of the Skookum­chuck Rapids, though at various stages of its history it was also located on the northeast side. The tiny community was called Skook­um­chuck till 1917, when Walter Wray applied for a post office designa­tion and called it Egmont.

Its official where­abouts depended on where the post office happened to be at the time. Controlling the mail meant life or death to a general store owner. At one point, in 1921, Walter Wray actually hijacked it from the then-postmaster George Hatashita, towing it by barge to his own place on the east shore. The struggle went on for years, with the post office being relocated so often that eventually Canada Post lost its patience and de­clared its final and irrevocable location to be Egmont, where it is now.

Egmont guarded its isolation from the world till 1956, when the road to the main highway was completed. But connecting to the outside hasn’t changed it much. Few people on their way to catch the Earl’s Cove ferry to Powell River take the time to follow this apparent road to nowhere. But for those who do, it’s a step into the past. You can have a beer at the Backeddy Pub or shop at the general store, just like folks have always done. And you’ll be welcome.
