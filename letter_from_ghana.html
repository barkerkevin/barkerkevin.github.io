Originally from Powell River, B.C., Jacqueline Murray is a Professor of History at the University of Guelph, and credits high school teacher Dick Hibberd (hello there Dick) with her lifelong obsession with history. She is passionate about promoting education for girls and women and has volunteered in Ghana on a number of occasions tasked with increasing literacy. Although she left the Sunshine Coast 40 years ago, it still inhabits her dreams.

 

July, 2013 | Dateline: Accra, Ghana. By Jacqueline Murray |


... Jacqueline Murray is the recipient of this year's 3M National Teaching Fellowship, widely considered to be Canada’s top teaching honour ...

Accra the capital of Ghana, formerly the Gold Coast, is a name that used to conjure dreams of gleaming wealth. While evocative of nineteenth-century colonialism, there’s not much gold about this coast anymore. Rather, Accra’s aspect is now tinged with red.
The dirt in Accra is ubiquitous and very, very red. During the rainy season, that dirt turns into oozing red mud and raging red rivers. During the much longer dry season, the red dirt becomes a red dust that coats absolutely everything. White or pastel clothing is out of the question: the red dust dyes the fabric, pooling around the seams in particular. It does not wash out. Hair is permeated and there are red swirls in the shampoo suds leaving a lingering doubt that the red dust ever really washes out.
Perhaps the very worst aspect of the red earth is the ant hills. Apparently, red dirt is particularly attractive to ants. These anthills are eight foot high towers, pushing up into the tree canopy, even incorporating and supporting full size trees growing from halfway up the hills. As arthropods go, the builders of these soaring anthills warrant their own series of science fiction movies. Every now and then, human beings exercise their dominance over nature, knocking down an anthill so the land can be repurposed. Do these acts of vandalism occasion heroic struggles between insect and human? It is difficult to be certain, but it would appear the humans prevail, at least for now.
Accra – and by extension Ghana – offers a layered experience. Layers of histories coexist with layers of identities. Remnants of many pasts intersect with presents from the global now. Take, for example, the ban on drumming that pertains in Accra in the May/June period; it dates to the prehistory of the Ga, the indigenous people of Accra.
Every year, in areas inhabited by the Ga, there is a six-week ban on drumming and noise-making which is mandated by the Ga Traditional Council. The ban is part of the observances of the Ga festival of Homowo, which means “to hoot at hunger.” Homowo commemorates a famine sometime in the deep past. It runs from May to late September and is celebrated to usher in the farming season. The ban is imposed on all inhabitants of Accra, Ga and non-Ga alike, and is part of the Ga’s traditional religious practices. Moreover, they argue that since indigenous customary practices are guaranteed by the 1992 Constitution of Ghana, all residents of Accra must respect the ban. And so, for a few weeks, the music is stilled and the drums lie silent as contemporary Accra bows to the imperatives of traditional society and marks a famine that once troubled the Ga.
From the perspective of a visitor, Accra is familiar and strange and perplexing. It is not an exotic capital city, such as Istanbul or Bangkok, nor is it a bustling power centre, such as Bejing or Washington. Accra is a crossroads of traditional, colonial and modern. All influence contemporary Accra and the essence of Ghanaian values, those values that have resulted in this country being perhaps the most stable democracy in all of Africa.
Religion is evident everywhere in Accra. Along virtually every road and highway, billboards blast out the apocalyptic calls to prayer of Christian charismatic and evangelical preachers, while the less strident sounds of the muezzin’s call to prayer waft across the city. Slightly less visible to the uninitiated, but saturating the culture, are the vestiges of traditional beliefs. Christianity is most obviously of the conservative ilk exported by the United States, but the denominations of colonialism also endure: Roman Catholic, Anglican, Presbyterian.
Only about 5% of Ghanaians declare their religious affiliation to be traditional, but traditional beliefs and values imbue life, particularly in the villages, and they also endure in the cities. Traditional beliefs, or at least cultural values and practices, cross the lines of religious and tribal identities. For example, an Ashanti Christian, living in Accra, performs traditional marriage rites, well in advance of the heavily westernized church wedding.
Tribal ethnic identities then are sufficiently deep and broad as to incorporate both Christians and Muslims, whether it be the Dagbamba of Upper East or Ewe people of Volta Region. The consequence of these religious and ethnic juxtapositions is that Ghana is remarkably free from religious intolerance and ethnic tensions. It is this harmony that sets in apart from so many neighbouring African countries, riven by ethnic and religious violence.
At every turn, the contradictions and complications are evident. This is a city, a country, that is fundamentally liminal. Ghana is striving to be a twenty-first-century country with nineteenth-century attitudes and twentieth-century tools. Much of the post-colonial 1960s infrastructure is beginning to crumble. In many ways, Accra is a city of deferred maintenance. The new George Walker Bush Motorway, the product of United States millennium generosity, is fed by arteries of crumbling pavement disappearing under the encroaching red dirt. Roadworks – the enduring British colonial term – are tackled with a shovel and pick axe, a wheelbarrow and sheer human muscle power. Here and there, fully-sealed air-conditioned condos are being built by men using cement mixers and bamboo scaffolding.
Disjunctions and intersections are visible everywhere. A tractor sits at the side of the road, the archaic Massey-Ferguson logo discernible through the red dust. To encourage farmers, tractors can be imported without duty, a significant concession on the part of the cash-strapped government. Urban gardens are everywhere in Accra, on boulevards, between houses, reclaiming garbage dumps. But these plots, large and small, bursting forth with corn and cabbage, peppers and tomatoes, have never been under a tractor’s plough. They are cultivated by backbreaking labour, with traditional short-handled hoes, no matter how large the plot.
The traditional short-handled hoe is complemented by the traditional twig broom, used by men and women both, to sweep houses, courtyards, even streets. The bundles of twigs are no more than two feet long and users bend over, sweeping with one hand, the other firmly behind their back. As gruelling as this kind of work may seem, there is a choice involved. There are long-handled mops used to wash floors and long handled shovels for digging ditches and roads.
There is an element of choice, to then, to the pervasiveness of traditional tools. This preference for the artefacts of the past is observed, too, in the endurance of traditional dress on the streets and in the offices of Accra and in the careful greetings and vestiges of colonial politesse that characterize social interactions.
How Ghanaians interweave their beliefs and their pasts, tribal and colonial, Christian, Muslim and traditional, informs how they navigate the globalized present, with a firm identity and without fear of cultural homogenization. Theirs is a strong and enduring culture; it is a dynamic culture one encounters daily on the dusty red streets of Accra.

Jacqueline Murray
