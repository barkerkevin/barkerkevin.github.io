A Ramble on Redrooffs Road

|by terry barker | 2:21 p.m. | February 12, 2014 |

This is the tenth in a series of columns of historical interest about places on the Sunshine Coast. It’s meant to help visitors and newbies get to know something about us, but it’s also useful to old-timers who’ve forgotten (or never learned) some of our stories.

There is a host of interesting communities between Sechelt and Madeira Park, not the least of which is West Sechelt, an amazing area which has grown like a volunteer field of daisies in recent years. But there are others with their own stories – Sargeant Bay, Coopers Green and Halfmoon Bay come to mind. Get off that damn highway, turn on to Redrooffs Road, and take your time to explore these still-rural places. It’s worth the trip.Sargeant Bay is your first stop. Watch for the sign and turn left. It’s a splendid provincial park, including a sand and gravel beach, a wetland, a salmon-bearing creek, forest, a pristine forest bog and well-planned connecting trails.

Frederick Sargeant pre-empted Sargeant Bay in 1887. He cleared the land and farmed for twenty years, but after he sold to new owners there was much dispute over the right use of the land, a natural habitat for birds and animals. The government settled the issue by turning it into a park in 1989.

All the shoreline from Welcome Beach to Halfmoon Bay James was acquired by James Rawding and Ephraim Walker in 1891.  Rawding sold his portion to a Vancouver firm in 1909, which constructed a summer resort called Redrooffs. The double “f” is the old English spelling for “red roofs”, a reference to the colour of the roofs of the cottages. They opened the Redrooffs Trading Post in 1912 and added a recreation hall in the 1930’s.

Jim Green bought it in 1955 and turned it into a trailer Park. The Sunshine Coast Regional District bought it and made it into a park called Coopers Green. The above drawing shows the community center. I’ve had many a happy day there, playing horseshoes and eating cotton candy with my grandchildren at the annual Halfmoon Bay country fair. My drawing today is of a hotdog stand that served us some wonderful goodies a few years ago. It was designed with a tow hitch, so I guess it’s sleeping in some back yard now.

Halfmoon Bay, the end of the road, is a community of almost 3,000 folks. It’s served by the famous Halfmoon Bay General Store, where you can mail a letter, buy a beer and eat a fabulous ice cream cone. There are also two art galleries and a variety of home studios which artists open to the visiting public.

If you’re feeling adventurous, go to the dock and rent a  kayak to the Merry Island lighthouse. It’s partly private property, and the other portion is home to one of Canada’s few remaining manned lighthouses. The island got its name from a survey conducted in 1865. HM Survey Ship Plumper was surveying the coast line and naming our salient features. The crew named Merry Islands after a winner of the Epsom Derby.

There are many stories about the heroism of Merry Island lighthouse keepers setting out in terrible storms to rescue people, and it makes me wonder how well a radar set would do under similar circumstances.
