Sweet Gibsons, Jewel of the Coast

This is the fourth in a series of columns of historical interest about places on the Sunshine Coast. It’s meant to help visitors and newbies to get to know something about us, but it’s also useful to old-timers who’ve forgotten (or never learned) some of our stories. Much of the contents of this column was originally printed in an earlier newspaper column and in Volume One of the Sunshine Sketches trilogy.

Gibsons and its environs was originally inhabited by the Chek-welps of the Squamish Nation. They called the area SK-JUNK, meaning “man watching” (from a rocky headland).

Francis Wyngaert, in his The West Howe Sound Story 1886-1976 (now out of print, alas), wrote that the first Europeans to settle here were George Gibson, a retired British naval lieutenant, and his two sons, George and Ralph. They pre-empted land and began farming, selling their produce to the rapidly growing city of Vancouver.

George Gibson was a real pioneer. He ran away from home in England in 1841 and went to sea. Later he worked on the Mississippi river boats, then went to Ontario and farmed. Finally he and his two sons came to B.C., where they searched for land they could pre-empt and farm.

They were blown off-course in their hand-made sloop, the Swamp Angel, and found themselves in what we now know as Gibsons harbour.  You can see his statue at the juncture of School Road, Gower Point Road and Gibsons Way in Lower Gibsons. He is remembered as a rather dour old chap, which is not surprising when you consider that he was 58 when he homesteaded here. He pre-empted and cleared land, and built a two-story home.

In the years following, the main sources of occupation were hand log­ging, shingle-bolting, fishing and farming.
Between 1903 and 1910, there was an “invasion” of Finnish families who set up socialist com­munes in Gibsons Heights, near what are now North Road and Cemetery Road. Their in­fluence was later felt in the ideas of J.S. Woods­worth, the founder of the CCF (now the New Democrat Party), who boarded for a time in the Gibsons home of Dr. Frederick Inglis.  Inglis was the only doctor on the Sunshine Coast for 33 years. His name is still revered by folks in these parts.

As Gibson’s Landing grew, so did the need for employ­ment. One of the most successful operations was the Howe Sound Co-operative Canning Association, a berry cannery that lasted from 1921 till 1952.

In 1929 the Village of Gibson’s Landing was incorporated.

In 1950 the name was amended to the Village of Gibsons, and it became known as the Town of Gibsons in 1983. Car ferry service between Horse­shoe Bay and Gibsons began in 1951, ending when B.C. Ferries moved the terminal to Langdale in 1959. Its population today is over 4,000.

Gibsons is known worldwide as the Home of the Beachcombers, C.B.C.’s long-running TV show. It ran for 19 years, and was seen in 45 countries. When I travel in Europe, and people ask me where I’m from, I always say “Gibsons” (even though I live in Sechelt), and they always respond with, “Oh, yes, the Beachcombers! Oh, I would love to live there!”
