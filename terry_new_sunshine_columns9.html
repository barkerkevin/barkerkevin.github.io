Memories of Wilson Creek

This is the sixth in a series of columns of historical interest about places on the Sunshine Coast. It’s meant to help visitors and newbies to get to know something about us, but it’s also useful to old-timers who’ve forgotten (or never learned) some of our stories.
Hey, traveler, if you're driving from Langdale to Earl's Cove today, you might miss Wilson Creek altogether. It's a small place, just a single road actually, with dead-end offshoots. You just might be forced to stop at the traffic light at Field Road, and you just might glance to the right and spy the IGA Mall and think about getting some groceries – or, looking at the stores, consider brewing some wine, eating a pizza, consulting a doctor, getting a prescription filled, renting a video, buying a gift, dressing for an adventure or scarfing a coffee -- but then the light turns green, off you go. In a hurry to get somewhere else.  No Wilson Creek for you today.
Enjoy the ride: the road from Wilson Creek to Sechelt was completed in 1912, and not paved till 1919. The rest of the highway, from Langdale to Earl's Cove, wasn't paved till the mid-fifties.
Like all the Sunshine Coast places, Wilson Creek has its own special interest and charm. It has an airport, a gated marina community, a salmon enhancement hatchery and a splendid campsite, as well as an interesting assortment of homes and industries.
I was first attracted to the area by The Homestead, a tiny log cabin which sat at the corner of Field and the highway many years ago. They served the best coffee and eggs-basted that I had ever eaten, well worth the drive from Gibsons. It was built in 1919 and sold to the Sunshine Coast Wildlife Rehabilitation Center for a dollar in 1992. They hauled its numbered logs to acreage on Trout Lake Road, where I found its remains in 1999. It had been reassembled by Harold Swanson and was being used to help rehabilitate hurt animals. It's gone from there now, but where? Like so many things on my beloved Coast, it has wandered off into limbo.
Wilson Creek was pre-empted by William Simpson, but for some reason it was named after a local blacksmith, Jim Wilson. He worked for Burns and Jackson Logging. It was the Jacksons who built that gracious house at the opposite corner from the Homestead in 1936, where they presided over their log boom storage area. You might have glimpsed it while you were stopped at that traffic light at Field and the highway, if you had looked left. Don’t look now, though – like The Homestead, it has gone.

Turn right, traveler, and spend some time at the Salmonid Hatchery. Go up Field Road till you see the sign. Follow the narrow gravel single-lane road down the hill till you find a magical opening in the forest where some dogged volunteers and a couple of employees raise thousands of salmon, steelhead and cutthroat trout and return them to Sunshine Coast rivers and streams. I talked with Bob Anstead, the amiable manager of the site, who has been running the program for 17 years. “We’re open to the public every day except Sunday,” he told me, “9:30 to 3:30 every day except Sundays. Tell your readers to come on down.”

Terry Barker is the author of Boss Talk, The Five Minute Police Officer, the Sunshine Sketches trilogy, A Walk in Snickett Park and Last Chance This Life. He has just published a book of his poetry called “two old broads and other poems”.
