This is the fifth in a series of columns of historical interest about places on the Sunshine Coast. It’s meant to help visitors get to know something about us, but it’s also useful to old-timers who’ve forgotten (or never learned) some of our stories. Some of the contents of this column were originally printed in an earlier newspaper column and in the Sunshine Sketches trilogy.

by terry barker |
Roberts Creek has one of the most colourful histories of any community on the Sunshine Coast. It's hard to know where to start.
Perhaps the story of Harry Roberts would be a good choice. He was known to at least one early Creeker as “Roberts Creek Harry”, when he gave her his autograph. The name fits him: he was the heart and soul of his community from 1904 till 1930, (or thereabouts â€“ no one knows for sure) when he pulled up stakes and sailed to Merry Island, where he built himself a small house, and later to Nelson Island, where he built “Sunray”, his last home. He built ships and rental cottages and even a castle on the beach, ran the post office and general store, built and operated a mill, wrote books and lived to be 100. Will we ever see his like again?
There are three different versions of the origin of the term, “Sunshine Coast”, but no one disputes that it was Harry who painted SUNSHINE BELT on the wall of his general store.
In the 1920’s, The Creek became a favourite summer haunt for Vancouver’s well-to-do. Mums and kids came to spend the holidays in Harry’s cabins, with Dads coming up for weekends on the famous Union Steamships “daddy boats”. The 200 acre Camp Byng on Lower Road was established during those years, too, as well as two other camps for boys and girls. There was a wild side to The Creek, too: let’s not forget that the Gumboot once was known as the Goon Saloon, where locals drank the home brew cooked up by the Amazons of the Crowe Road Herb Farm commune.
By the 30’s there was not only a general store, but also a post office building, beauty parlour, barber shop, shoe repair, summer resorts, bakery and service station. The community hall was built in 1934 and still flourishes today. I’ve spent many happy hours there, attending concerts or taking part in the famous annual craft fair.
The hippies arrived in the 60’s. They came with flowers in their hair, turning The Creek into our own mini-Haight-Ashbury. Their flavours and colours last to this day. I think the annual Creek Daze and the Piggly Wiggly Parade are direct descendants of the sweet life of those days. There are more artists and musicians per square meter than anywhere else in North America, not barring Taos.

Roberts Creek has changed radically since Harry Roberts built his sailboat, the Chack-Chack, on the beach, but I think he would approve of the texture and pizzazz that you feel every time you enter the Gumboot or check out the Mandela down at the pier (see drawing). Creekers are proud of their home, and protect its identity fiercely. No big developers welcome here! Anyone who tried to bulldoze large areas to build condos had better bring an army with them!
