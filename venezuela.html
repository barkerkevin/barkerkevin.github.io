by kevin barker

March, 2013 | Dateline: San Cristobal, Venezuela. By Kevin Barker |



I've always marvelled at the stoic silence the free world has maintained over Hugo Chavez. There have been no cries of regime change from Washington, no coalitions of the willing rolling across the oil fields towards Caracas. Not even a recent peep from the U.S. State Department about election fraud.

What's the reason for this cordon sanitaire of averted eyes and stilled voices? Is it merely the icy deference that rational people characteristically bestow on the mildy insane? There is an extremely reckless theory that maybe Chavez wasn't all that bad and there just wasn't much to object to, but nobody seems to want to go there.

I think it's plain indifference. The last time the northern hemisphere paid attention to South America, taking it at face value, we became intrinsically involved in a brutal 20 year dictatorship in Chile and waged a short, sharp war over some islands off Argentina. Sure, the dictadura was good for transnationals, but nobody in their right mind wants a repeat of it. We live in a kinder, gentler world now, where Obama's drones do all the dirty work.

At the time of the Falklands War, which our heads of state generously referred to as Las Malvinas at last year's disastrous summit in Cartagena, Colombia, we still had apartheid. Something the British government was more or less tacitly ok with back in those dark days. Not so now.

The last good look I had at Chavez and South American politics en toto was a televised, pan Caribbean presidential summit a few years back. It was quite a show. Daniel Ortega produced a hand-drawn map of San Andres, a Colombian archipelago off the coast of Nicaragua, and said rather brusquely that his country wanted it back. Colombia's Álvaro Uribe, the former strongman president who is now engaged in a relentless Twitter battle with the current leadership, produced a series of emails retrieved from the laptop of a recently slain FARC commandante which detailed wire payments to Ecuador president Rafael Correa in exchange for safe haven. Uribe had just worked himself up into a lather when Chavez interrupted and began to sing. Everyone laughed and it was smiles until tea time. Hey, this is South American bilateralism. I'm not making it up.

So it's not hard to imagine why the southern Hemisphere gets little more than a collective yawn from the north. Even the million dollar question posed today by the El Spectador newspaper, "will the Chavista revolution survive?" is rather academic. A potentially better question is whether Chavez has a real shot at martyrdom. Martyrdom needs a conspiracy, like the one generated by Chavez himself over the mysterious demise of South American liberator Simon Bolivar.

I said I didn't believe the CIA murdered Chavez. I didn't say he wasn't murdered.

You also need sweeping, beneficient acts, acknowledged by your peers. Colombian president Juan Manuel Santos used the ubiqitous Twittersphere today to issue condolences to Venezuelans, and came tantalizingly close to recognizing a Chavez accomplishment or two. Among those are the severe reduction in extreme poverty and infant mortality, which our governments are reluctantly forced to admit were kind of ok. Not the sort of thing that gets recognition from banks and oil companies, the people who count. But they did happen.

He also had friends on Wall Street, including Goldman Sachs and OppenheimerFund Inc, both earning up to 14.7% annually (aggregate 681% over the decade!) on Venezuelan bonds by playing the ongoing fiscal crises rather expertly. You see, Chavez was willing to siphon off the country’s oil wealth to pay creditors, as a way to counter start/stop growth, falling reserves, and the dearth of foreign investment. The nationalization policies of the Chavistas that kept investors away in droves resulted in a whopping 12% in borrowing costs - a full four points points higher than other developing nations. High anxiety means high bond yields. But contrary to popular predictions the country did not land in default. Indeed, he never missed a payment. Those guys made out like bandits on Chavez.

So just what is the world's problem? Despite countless conversations with expatriate Venezuelans over the past 10 years, one of them an army colonel involved in the illfated 2002 coup in Caracas, (hello there Charles), I have yet to gather a tangible reason for their vehement hatred of Chavez. Yeah, he was a dictator. That's bad I guess. What did he really do? Well he dictated. Tsk. Ok, what else? High crime in Caracas, expropriations, tough money laws, plummeting currency. Well the latter two aren't so bad. In fact my own native land - Canada - would fare extremely well from a currency devaluation: The factories would run 24/7 and we'd have full employment.

You could say, as some New York based human rights watchdogs do, that he played fast and loose with constitutional law and rigged elections. Doesn't everybody these days? I mean come on. And where is the evidence linking Chavez to murder, embezzlement, and treason? Where are the victims and the families of victims? Where are the stories of fighter jets strafing refugees on makeshift rafts, the revelations in the world press about offshore accounts and wire transfers and webs of interlocking companies, like Aleman in Nicaragua, and Fujimori and Montesino in Peru, and Cheney in the U.S.? Where are the whistleblowers?

I think hating Chavez is the most bourgeois thing you can do. His common man speech, his Mussolini like posturing, and the endless Castro style speeches were calculated to get on the nerves of certain people. Banks and industries were nationalized, people were thrown out of work, and it became next to impossible to move money around. No, that's not the United States I'm talking about silly! But it sounds like home dunnit?

This just in ... apparently the Koch brothers murdered Chavez.

Now that I can believe.
