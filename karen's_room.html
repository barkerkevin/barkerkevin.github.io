This is Karen’s room.

Long before we built the house, when we were still in the wouldn’t it be nice to have a house we designed ourselves stage, she said she wanted her own room. A room where she could read, where she could write, paint, sew and think.


“It has to have morning light,” she said. “And I have to see the sun set on Vancouver Island from it. And it has to have a daybed.” I was never sure why it had to have a daybed; but if Karen wanted a daybed, it would have one. I would give Karen anything she wanted. She held down two jobs to put me through law school; I owed her everything.
She told me I was a great lawyer, although I never thought I was. She told other people I was too, and they believed her because everyone always believed Karen. She told someone that at one of those dreary lawyer parties in Vancouver and a week later I got a phone call.
“We’re looking for someone with your specialty,” the authoritative voice on the telephone said, “Why don’t you come up and see us?”
And it was up. Very very up. The firm liked me and they loved Karen. Within two years we were thinking that our own house was a possibility. Within four years, we were looking at property on the Sunshine Coast.
“The view is spectacular,” the real estate agent said. “It’s about 300 feet down to the ocean, so I wouldn’t count on daily swims; but from the prominence, you can see the sunrise over there and the sunset about there…”
The next morning, we huddled in down jackets in the pre-dawn mist. The morning sun slivered though the clouds at the head of the inlet and caught Karen’s smile in its golden glow. As the sun burst over the cloud bank, she danced and her shadow slid over the massive expanse of granite we knew would hold our new home. We stayed there until the last embers of the sunset burned out on Vancouver Island. We couldn’t get back to our apartment fast enough to the real estate lady.
The first drawings were on the backs of envelopes and shopping lists. By the time we graduated to typing paper and graph paper, cedar beams, stone fireplaces and rustic staircases came and went: their inclusion and exclusion was debated. But the floor-to-ceiling glass windows were never an issue. “It will be hard to heat,” the architect said. “The storms will blow straight at that southwest corner where those big windows are.” Karen just shook her head. “No, that room will be warm.”
And it is. I can sit here now – at her writing desk or on the daybed or in the teak sling chairs she loved to curl up in – and feel the first warmth of the sun in the morning. I can see that morning-orange light illuminate her book cases and paintings all around me. As the sun moves out toward the ocean, the room fills with the crystal blue light of day that inspired her writing. And when day is done, the butterscotch sunset light floods this room with the warmth and love we knew here.
The last, sangria light of the day hits the corkboard that’s overpopulated with pushpins holding photographs, letters, postcards and, in the bottom right hand corner, a note in her handwriting that just says “thank you.” It was the last thing she wrote before she died.
This is Karen’s room.
