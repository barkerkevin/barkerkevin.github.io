Karen's Room

 

 

Erkin | by jeff keller

Inspector Spall rode the shoeway to the banquet room where the Gost (according to the Curator of Schedules), was in the process of ingesting its midday meal. Sunlight shone, as it always did, through the crystal ceiling, and Spall louvered his eyelids until all but a halfmoon of pupil was shielded from the glare of the tablecloths. Spall disengaged from the shoeway and slowed his momentum with a practiced quickstep, then shaded his eyes with a gloved hand and panned his vision in a level arc across the vast and empty table-land.

There were tiny knots, saw Spall, of protoplastic, mineral, vegetative, energetic, inferred and hybrid lifeforms, clustered loosely around tables or tightly around troughs or singly at feeding tubes, depending on morphology and custom. Each small cluster formed the hub of a wheel, its spokes traced dynamically by bus-boys, waiters and Assistant Victuallers, a thousand crawling sychophants, each in the Grape and Ochre, VisHosp holograms proudly bowing on the epaulettes. It should all have amounted to a pretty sight, thought Spall, but each wheel formed only a lonely and disconnected archipelago in the grand expanse of the banquet room, which had been raised, thanks to low-gravity engineering, higher and wider than anything on earth, an immense and somewhat pompous structure designed to both feed and impress the hungry hordes of extra-terrestrial diplomats and sightseers predicted by the contact team. (But the throngs, intoned some bitter member of Spall's inner-most council, had never come.)

Spall's sources had suggested the northeastern periphery, and it was here he concentrated his gaze. In time he was able to make out a party of one in that general direction. Although it was too far away to make out features, the seated figure displayed a tiny bump to one side, and otherwise fit the subject's description (again, as provided by the Curator of Schedules).

Spall engaged his shoe and stepped onto a northbound subduction circuit, which drew him at about twice the speed of walking in the appropriate direction. As he glided forward he passed between rows of empty tables, rarely or never used, but adorned, every day, with a brave, if pointless, flower from earth.

When Spall was a half-minute's walk to the Gost's table he disengaged his shoe and stepped off the circuit, proceeding towards the contact point at a dignified pace. As he walked he peered surreptitiously at the Gost, and especially at the service and dishes laid out for the alien's repast.

The malaise that had twisted Spall's guts since his visit to the Cellar-General's office amplified when he noted that the delegate was avoiding the proschtl, which he identified by its large silver chafing dish. He worked his way closer still, then sidled up beside an Assistant Victualler.

“May your service sparkle and shine,” whispered Spall. “I have come to speak with the Gost. Is there a problem with the proschtl?”

The Assistant Victualler twitched a muscle behind his ear, and imperceptibly stiffened his neck.

“I haven't enquired,” he replied. “It's not my place.”

“Then unstiffen your neck,” suggested Spall, “and tell me what you've observed. No proschtl – no treaty. No treaty, no alliance. No alliance, and we can sit here like a cartful of pastries and wait for the Meidr to come.” The Assistant Victualler relented. He spoke slowly, at all times keeping his eyes on the Visitor, using hand gestures to command his scurrying boys.

“First it lifted the lid,” whispered the Victualler. “Then it sniffed and poked the proschtl. Then it....”  There was a pause, and Spall could almost hear the Victualler's mental clockwork ticking as he rifled through his vocabulary. “He ululated.” The Assistant Victualler's lips compressed. “One of the trencher lads broke down and cried – right there at the table. Completely ruined for Service.”

“I'm sure he'll be fine in Kitchen,” said Spall. He left the Assistant Victualler and approached the banquet table. Although he kept his attention focused on his target, he walked slowly, taking advantage of his wide-set eyes to surreptitiously inspect the proschtl. It seemed ordinary enough. A bit, perhaps, like ham, but tinged blue. And how, he wondered, was he supposed to inspect a dish he had never eaten, never seen, never read the recipe for? He took three steps towards the Gost and bent forward to indicate a desire to speak. The delegate noticed him and grew still.

“Begging your pardon, Visitor,” said Spall, adorning his normally severe countenance with the VisHosp Smile of Concern. “Was there a problem with the proschtl?”

The Gost rose up from the table and bowed, clumsily, but without reservation. As it spoke, the diminutive Annunciator squatted by its side, working the portable bellows that allowed the Gost to form human sounds by focusing air through its vens; tentall gilled breathing tubes that rose and swayed from the back of of the Gost's neck like a fan of cobras, or the pipes of a medieval organ.

“Begging your pardon, human,” sang the Gost in chords, “but this is not proschtl. Proschtl is a different dish. Very tender, very sweet, transcendent – not like this. We are aware of the trouble you went to, but your food-implementers have failed. This is odd, since all but the skill were supplied. The skill, of course, and the erkin.”

“Erkin?” asked Spall. “I'm afraid I didn't bring a linguist. Would you mind explaining erkin?”

“Erkin is erkin,” said Ten-prime. “Erkin is...qualitative, ineffable, non-material. Even when the war is over, and the telmars are incoherent, you must still find a thrum to keep the erkin in. Do you see? But who can find a thrum? Only he who can make a thrum can find a thrum. That which is predicable of erkin is predicable of thrum, and both have attributes which are neither here nor there, but somewhere in-between.”

Spall's confusion was apparent, even across the species-divide. Ten-prime's vens waved airily as he spoke.

“Do not concern yourself with thrum, human – first you must identify erkin. Look to your cooking, which may be the true special talent. My guess is that you will find erkin in your kitchens. All things considered, it is strange that you cannot make proschtl. In general, your cooking is advanced. Once we have defeated the Meidr, we will discuss the importation of foodstuffs to Gost. We are particularly fond of cake. But for now, human, you must learn to make proschtl in time for the ceremony. This is important, if you wish to gain our help against the Meidr. If you cook with erkin, all the better.”

The Gost performed its strange side-to-side bowing procedure, ululated briefly, then made an eerie and protracted hissing noise before turning again to consume its meal. Spall, his face creased with worry, ordered the proschtl taken away, then followed the servant into the kitchen to confer with Chef Lakh.

ll

After fifty-three years of ruling over VisHosp's kitchen, Chef Lakh was beginning to show his age. He walked with a cane, dozed off between services, and spoke from the hip. In the matter of proschtl, he chose not to waste energy feigning ignorance.

“I knew there'd be an issue,” he snapped, clacking his square yellow teeth together like ochred castanets. “Frankly, proschtl goes far beyond our job description. Do you have any idea what a proscht is, Inspector? Do you have any idea how to prepare proschtl?”

“I haven't a clue,” said Spall. “I only learned of proschtl yesterday when the Cellar-General asked me to look in on  today's luncheon. Quite frankly, Chef Lakh, we have a problem. Small delinquencies in service can have enormous repercussions – in this case, what hangs in the balance is the good will of the Gost in our defence against the Meidr. What I understand from CultSynch, and from the Gost, is that the serving of well-prepared proschtl is absolutely essential to a successful treaty negotiation. No matter how difficult or abhorrent the process, I cannot impress upon you strongly enough that it simply, for the ongoing good of humanity, needs to be performed.”

“Then I think,” said the Chef, “that you need to meet the proscht. Would you follow me please, Inspector?”

“This is it, Inspector,” said Lakh. “But keep your voice down, if you don't mind. Chef Reedle only just got him to sleep.” Lakh reached up and pulled away the tarp. Inside the cage lay the proscht, sleeping, its plump blue body strapped to a board.

Spall turned expectantly towards the towering Cooler complex at the back of the Kitchen, monolithic refrigeration units built to house the cuisines of as many (according to the Greeters) as ten thousand planetary cultures. Many of those, as on earth, boasted hundreds, or even thousands of  tribal subcultures, each with its own cuisine. As of yet, however, the Coolers only stored the foods of seven worlds, a situation that mirrored, in general, earth's status on the galactic priority list.

“Not the Coolers, Sir,” said Lakh. “We've been keeping the proscht outside, in the stockyard, next to the flickers.”

"The proscht are alive?” asked Spall.

“It certainly is,” replied Lakh. “The proscht is alive, and healthy, and ready for preparation.” Chef Lakh opened the kitchen door and led Spall outside, tapping his cane down the long, wood-planked walkway, past cages filled with the extraterrestrial animals that VisHosp imported for its guests from other worlds.

Down past the dovecotes, which caged innumerable iterations of the deadly Artundian monoflicker, and past the strink-pond, overgrown with voracious but succulent water-wraiths, then around and by the side of the third great barn, was an ordinary square cage, just big enough for a small man to enter and lie down.

“This is it, Inspector,” said Lakh. “But keep your voice down, if you don't mind. Chef Reedle only just got him to sleep.” Lakh reached up and pulled away the tarp. Inside the cage lay the proscht, sleeping, its plump blue body strapped to a board.

“Lakh,” said Spall, “that can't be the proscht?”

“That is the proscht, Inspector. It was delivered, with proper documentation and labelling, by the Deep-Jump InToStella, which received the parcel from the Gost Interchange. He arrived with instructions, extra ingredients, and, unexpectedly, an instrument called the zenproschten, which you can just see peeking out from behind the cage to your left.”

Lakh indicated a dull grey box about the size of a child's coffin, then stepped to the side of the cage, reached down and pulled it towards Spall. “When you're done inspecting the proscht, I will explain the zenproschten to you.”

“I don't want to get any closer to the proscht than I have to,” said Spall. “Especially since it looks like a....”

“Yes,” said Lakh. “It does.”

Spall knelt down, close enough to smell the dirt and sour hay of the stockyard floor. The figure in the cage had an unpleasantly familiar shape – two arms, two legs, five palp-tipped digits on each hand. But where the swaddling left skin uncovered it glistened with an oily, blue, iridescent patina.

Spall stood. He looked off into the distance, pretending to consider, when in reality he was attempting to bully his brain past a perceptual impasse.

“Chef Lakh,” he said, to buy himself thinking time, “explain the zenproschten.”

“Very well, Inspector,” said Lakh, his mouth twisting over his ogre's teeth. “My pleasure.”

The Chef knelt by the zenproschten and touched two square bosses on the lid, which sprang back to reveal an odd assemblage of pans, springs, levers, cogs, wheels, rotors, hydraulics, needles, servos, vacuums and blades. Lakh removed a succession of pans, each ornately perforated with lace-like patterns, to reveal a final, womb-like indentation that was shaped to encompass the proscht, which would, explained Lakh, be placed face down inside the zenproschten with its head outside one end of the box, its feet out the other.

“Sinister,” said Spall.

“Sinister indeed,” agreed Lakh, pulling a sheet of instructions from a cavity in the zenproschten. “As you can imagine, there isn't a chef in the restaurant who's prepared to do the strangling – not for any bonus.  And even before that, there's the stril – here they are – they look like sharpened slivers of bamboo. The recipe specifies that sixty stril must be larded into the living proscht's muscle tissue. By the way, if you touch a stril, don't lick your fingers or touch your eyes till you've washed. As Chef Deepak discovered, they are at least four times as hot as anything we grow on earth. And by the way, the recipe advises the chef to strap the proscht to the cutting board before attempting to insert the stril, as the proscht, and I quote, 'will inevitably struggle and cry.'”

“I've heard enough, Lakh,” said Spall. “Tell your chefs that as far as I'm concerned they will not be asked to...prepare...the proschtl.”

Lakh bowed. “Thank you, Inspector. We are cooks, not murderers. The relief will run high, and the spirits will run free. Toasts will be raised to your wisdom.”

Lakh took a flask from his back pocket, waved it in Spall's direction and took a long pull.

“By the way,” asked Spall. “What was the proschtl you served tonight?”

“It was a ham, Sir,” said Lakh. “An old-fashioned, unsmoked ham that we soaked in blue dye. According to Access, there used to be people called 'cannibals' who ate human flesh, which they referred to as 'long-pig.' Given the similarities, I took a chance. Perhaps it's time to hang up my cap. If proschtl becomes a regular menu item, I don't think I want to cook here anymore.”

“I don't think there's much danger of that,” said Spall. “However, we are playing a desperate game here, Lakh. Somehow we need to serve the Gost a feast of proschtl, or we stand to lose the war against the Meidr.”

The proscht opened its eyes, shrugged sleepily, then focused on Lakh and smiled. Its golden-eyed gaze turned to Spall, and the smile disappeared. Looking away, it struggled against the swaddling, yawned, made a noise that might have been a fart and subsided into sleep again.

Lakh grimaced. “If there's killing to be done, you'll have to do it yourself, Inspector, or find a man without heart or conscience. There isn't a cook in the kitchen who wouldn't rather be chastised, demoted, or even expelled.”

“I can't say I blame them,” said Spall. “By the way, is there anything in that recipe that corresponds to the word erkin?”

“Erkin, Inspector? Lakh poked through the instruction sheet. “It's not on the list.”

“Never mind, then. It's just that the Gost told me we lacked sufficient erkin to make a proper proschtl.”

“I've never heard of erkin before,” said Lakh, “but if it's edible, we can make it, even if we have to synthesize it. But as I said, the word erkin does not appear in the recipe, or in the instructions for the zenproschten.”

Spall sighed and wiped sweat from his brow. He couldn't bring himself to blame the chefs, and there was no question of preparing the proschtl himself. He would need time to think this over.

lll

That evening, after the cheese plate, Spall brought the problem of proschtl to the attention of the Cellar-General, a salty old man who had earned his Fourchette some sixty years ago. Half an hour later they were standing in the stockyard, by the proscht cage.

“Unbelievable,” said the Cellar General. “It's quite simply unbelievable. Look at the way your flashlight reflects off its skin. Are you thinking what I'm thinking?”

Spall nodded. “It's the only answer that fits, Sir. We've only seen one Meidr, the one that declared war on us, but I'm pretty sure it looked like a larger version of that.” Spall indicated the sleeping proscht.

“Then where the hell did the Gost get it?”

“According to Chef Lakh,” replied Spall, “the provenance is clear – it was delivered to us from the Gost Interchange and travelled here under supervision on the Deep-Jump InToStella.”

The Cellar General's knees creaked as he bent down to look closer.

“Open the cage, Spall. Is it dangerous?”

“I don't think so, Sir. Not any more than one of our own. The cooks have been entering the cage every few hours to clean and feed it. So far there have been no reports of injury.”

Spall undid the latch and reached in to grasp the swaddling board. Its occupant sighed sleepily as the tiny bed was dragged across the dirt to the cage door. The Cellar General reached out, picked the proscht up in his hands and slowly stood straight again. He examined the little body at arm's length, then up close. The proscht stirred, struggled, and made a noise like a cry. The Cellar General looked startled, then his features softened and he cradled the proscht in the crook of his arm, with its head just under his chin.

“Spall, I heard what you said, and of course I believe you, but I'm still having a hard time believing that the Gost eat their enemy's children. Bring your nose over here – it even smells like a baby.”

“I wouldn't know, Sir,” replied Spall, holding his hand up to fend off the proscht. “My experience of children is limited. And besides, I don't want to know that it smells like a baby, because CultSynch's directions are clear: the proschtl must be served.”

The Cellar-General shook his head. “We need more information, Spall. Let's return to my office, remove the poor little thing from this instrument of torture, and take a proper look at its anatomy. I don't know about you,” he added, softly, “but I couldn't kill one of these, any more than I could kill one of our own.”

IV

Commander Hornbill at CultSynch had a sharp, no-nonsense attitude that perfectly matched the sour expression on his smug, handsome face.

“The proschtl is made from Meidr babies? That doesn't sound likely.”

“Granted,” said the Cellar-General. “It makes a lousy proposition, but there it is. It's a baby, and it looks strikingly like the image we saw of the Meidr. It appears to be personable and intelligent, but we're supposed to kill it and cook it. Frankly, Commander, we don't know what to do.”

“Nor do I,” said Hornbill. “These allegations are very inspecific. Frankly, it sounds like your entire department has come down with the collywobbles.”

“I assure you, Sir,” said the Cellar-General, “that the collywobbles are not a factor.”

“Be that as it may,” said Hornbill, “you're operating with an insufficiency of information. If I were you I would take counsel from the BookWorms.”

“That could take days,” said Spall. “At least give us High Access.”

Hornbill scowled. “With the Meidr coming, Inspector, Access is in extremely high demand. Quite frankly, in the absence of corroborative evidence, CultSynch is unable to help. This is a catering issue, Cellar-General, and I speak for the entire human race when I tell you that the proschtl must be served.”

The Access screen went black and the Cellar-General turned to Spall.

“And there we go. That's about what I expected.”

“What now?” asked Spall.

“Clenna Pfade of the Bookworms might be able to help us, but every time we go for assistance to another department we lose face. We're only VisHosp, and VisHosp is just service, so our problems, by definition, are insignificant, and so, by extension, are we. Except that they're not, and we're not. Listen, Spall, forget the BookWorms. Let's try our own research department.”

Spall looked puzzled.

“You mean Recipes?”

“Yes, I mean Recipes,” snapped the Cellar-General. “And don't stand there with that puzzled look on your face. Emma Tang may not be a BookWorm, but she's a smart young woman with a broad understanding of intergalactic customs. In addition, I seem to remember that she had children of her own before joining the Brotherhood. See what she thinks of this...proschtl ceremony.”

The Cellar-General looked at the squirming proscht and motioned peremptorily to Spall.

“I know you're not comfortable with babies, but this is work. Take it to Emma Tang and see if she can help. And remember, if word of this gets around it will put the negotiations in jeopardy. Be discrete, be efficient, be gone.”

Spall stood a moment, reverberating irresolutely in the aftermath of his dismissal, anger and confusion alternately draining and suffusing his features of blood. Then he sighed, scooped up the Meidr infant, bowed, and left the Cellar-General's office, turning towards the basement stairs and the dark underground office of the Recipes Department.

V

Emma Tang raised a supercilious eyebrow and looked down her long nose, through which issued an almost-equine snort of incredulity.

“That's a weird-looking baby. It's a blue, shiny-skinned, weird-eared baby.”

“Yes, thank you,” said Spall. “That much, in my capacity as Inspector, I've managed to ascertain.”

There was a pause. Then: “Seriously? You're asking me for a recipe for baby?” Emma Tang's eyebrows arched so high that Spall had to choke back a smile.

“Not exactly,” said Spall, easing off, just a little. “In fact, we already have a recipe for baby. I was wondering if you could take a look at it and...comment.”

“You want me to comment on a recipe for baby? You're going to cook this...poor little blue baby? You can't be serious.” Spall had worn the edge off her hauteur – she sounded almost plaintive.

“I wish I weren't,” sighed Spall. He handed her the recipe that Chef Lakh had given him. “This was presented to VisHosp as the recipe for proschtl. The main ingredient is proscht, which you see before you. As you noticed, it looks a lot like a human baby, but its skin is reflective and its ears are distinctly alien. From what we saw during the Meidr's Declaration of war, we think it's one of them.”

Emma leaned forward and sniffed. “It smells like a human baby.”

“So I've been told,” said Spall. “I've also been told that we need to slaughter it for the proschtl, to ensure a treaty with the Gost and protect the future of humanity.”

“Well I guess if you have to,” said Emma. “I mean, it's not human, so it's not exactly cannibalism. If it's intelligent, though, it's still murder. Don't ask me to do the cooking.”

“That's the other part of the problem,” said Spall. “What I'm about to tell you is a Level One Confidence.”

“Nothing is mine to give away,” murmured Emma.

“Then brace yourself, because this isn't pretty. It's called a zenproschten.”

When Spall had finished relating the preparation process for proschtl, Emma Tang sat back in her chair and scowled. “That doesn't sound right. It's...barbaric.”

“Agreed,” said Spall with a nod. “It's callous and seemingly primitive. Is there a single recipe for a ceremonial dinner in the culinary repertoire of any intergalactic civilization that involves eating the infants of an intelligent species?”

“Not in my experience,” said Emma. She crossed her legs, and for a quarter second all Spall could hear was the sound of fabric sliding across her thighs. “But I can't answer you for sure. We know almost nothing about what goes on outside our solar system.”

Spall's fingers formed a steeple, the tip of his index fingers just touching the tip of his nose. Despite his initial reaction to Emma she was beginning to shake his head out of the clouds. “An entire universe out there, and we know next to nothing about it. The Visitors have complete Access to our libraries, but if we want to investigate the origins of the zenproschten we can suck eggs.”

“Don't let Audit catch you,” said Emma. “Every egg we get up here costs thirty units to transport.” While Spall had been talking she'd opened a new enquiry. “Damn it, if I could get even a millisecond of Access I might be able to.... This is important, right?”

“The Gost won't sign a treaty without proschtl,” said Spall. “Therefore the proschtl must be served or earth will have no ally.”

Emma stared at him a long moment. “And it's down to us? Here in the basement of VisHosp?”

Spall grimaced, then leaned forward towards Emma and lowered his voice. “The Cellar-General talked to CultSynch. CultSynch said it's a catering problem.”

“I wish that surprised me,” said Emma. “But it doesn't. When it comes to bureaucratic myopia CultSynch takes the entire fucking cake.”

“I'd forgotten about the cake,” said Spall.

Emma looked puzzled. “What cake?”

Spall looked around a moment, then decided to sit. “I don't think it has any bearing. Except, perhaps, in a peripheral fashion. “What's bothering me is something the Gost said. It may be entirely irrelevant, but he implied that if humans were to travel outside our solar system, we should do so on the basis of cake. He dismissed everything we do as simple or trite...except for cake.”

“Well we do make good cakes,” said Emma. “At least there's something special about us. And besides, you're in the cake business yourself, you know, if only peripherally.”

“I suppose you're right,” said Spall. “But...cake?”

Emma shrugged. “Why not? Better that than proschtl.”

Emma turned back to her Access panel, entered a Recipe Department high-priority demand code, and hardly had time to pout her lips into an expectant mouee before the information she'd requested was up on the screen.

“There was something like the zenproschten in ancient France,” she summarized. “They called it a duck press. The duck was strangled to keep the blood inside the body. After it was roasted, the breasts and legs were removed and the rest of the carcass was put in the press, along with wine, cognac, and a variety of optional ingredients. The fluids, and by fluids I mean blood and bone marrow, were squeezed out to make a sauce. It's a variation of the same process used in the making of proschtl, don't you think?”

“Similar,” said Spall. “But I'm not sure that tells us anything.”

“It tells us one thing,” said Emma. “Historically, we had a similar food-preparation process,
but it didn't survive the 21st century. We outgrew it, the same way we outgrew torture and war.”

“What does that tell us?”

“Some emotional reactions are inherent and axiomatic,” said Emma, “and the Gost aren't idiots. I'd put money on them knowing that proschtl is contrary to both our social conditioning and our biological instincts. In my opinion, for what that's worth, we're either being tested or teased.”

“Thank you,” sighed Spall. “That's more-or-less the idea that's been trying to form in my head all day. But why would they do it? And why would they value the actions of VisHosp, which, let's face it, doesn't exactly represent the pinnacle of human achievement.”

“Maybe that's it,” said Emma. “Maybe they want to test us in the middle of the curve. Or maybe they really do consider cake the pinnacle of human achievement.”

Spall sat silent a moment as the implications sank in. “I think you're right. It must be a test. And not just the proschtl. Why restrict your logic regarding scientific achievement and civilization to the Gost? Perhaps we should be asking why the Meidr declared war on us in the first place.”

“True enough,” said Emma. “It makes even less sense than the proschtl. Could a people advanced enough to build galaxy-class technology still engage in wars of conquest? What's the motivation? And why wouldn't the other races join together to shut them down?”

“Again,” said Spall, “you're making some dangerous assumptions. And yet...”

“And yet what?” huffed Emma.

“Can you access the Meidr declaration of war?”

“I can try,” she replied. Emma turned to Access and tapped her fingers across the board. An image appeared. In the foreground was a small blue man, sitting in what was clearly the captain's chair of an alien vessel.

“We are the Meidr,” said the image on the screen. “We come from the eleventh planet of a star for which you have  no name or number.” He snapped his fingers and the orange globe of an unknown planet filled a screen behind his head. “In several months we will reach your solar system.” The blue man smiled beatifically and bowed. “Please consider this a declaration of war.” The transmission ended.

“Short, concise and polite,” said Spall. “It hardly seems like a declaration of war at all. There's no weight to it, no reason, no gravitas.”

“No human gravitas,” replied Emma. “Perhaps if we were Meidr or Gost we'd be pissing ourselves in terror.”

“I suppose that's true,” said Spall. “But we're still left with the same basic problem – what could the Meidr want from us that would be worth travelling all the way from...wherever they come from?”

Spall happened to be looking at Emma's eyes, and watched with detachment as her lids flicked open and her pupils dilated with fear.

“What if....” Emma hunched over the desk and lowered her voice. “What if they're planning to eat us?”

Spall chuckled and shook his head. “I doubt it. Not if this infant is any reflection of the adult Meidr. Its diet consists of carbon-based analogues of our grains, fruits and vegetables. Its gut is doubled, like a ruminant's, and its grinding apparatus, which I hesitate to call teeth, is not even remotely evolved for meat.”

They spent a moment looking at the sleeping Meidr, which rolled and mewled and waved its arms in contrary motion. Its stomach quaked, and the room filled with the sharp smell of hay.

“Which part of the stockyard does that remind you of?” asked Spall, dryly.

“The barn,” admitted Emma. She picked the Meidr infant up and snuggled it to her chest. “Even if he weren't a vegetarian, I'd have a problem imagining this little butterball as a killer. In fact,” she added, “I think he's more of a lover than a fighter.” The infant, Spall noted, had rotated in her arms, and was making sucking noises with its mouth as it spread its finger-palps across her breast.

“Perhaps the Meidr breastfeed?”

“What are they, then? Little blue alien mammals?” Emma looked sceptical.

“Why not? This one has roughly mammalian genitals.”

“Well they don't look anything like my mammalian genitals,” said Emma. “And if he's looking for milk, he sure won't find any here.”

“We could induce lactation chemically,” said Spall. “As a...cross-species culinary experiment.”

Emma stared at him coldly. “If you're being lubricious, Inspector, I might remind you that we already have an inter-special culinary experiment on our hands. Perhaps one is enough for the moment?”

Spall smiled guiltily. “I apologize. To tell the truth, I'm exhausted. I've been going all day on a bowlful of Rise. If you don't have plans, would you like to come back to my cubicle for dinner?”

“Why not stay here?” Emma reached behind her to pull a cord which released a drape that hid a comfortable cubicle with bed, toilet and kitchen. “I have norwits, shu-shu, and, if you promise to tell absolutely no one, a bottle of Frizz. They are, if anybody asks, ingredients.”

Spall raised his eyebrows. “Norwits, shu-shu and Frizz? I should have been in Recipes. I suppose I ought to report you for Sequestering, but frankly I'd rather have dinner.”

“Food and rules are a terrible mix,” said Emma. “Abandon all honour, ye who wish to be fed. Now if you're serious about wanting to be in Recipes, uncork the Frizz and pour me a glass – you might just get your wish.”

Vl

The Recipes department, it turned out, was the haven of excellent chefs – none more-so than Emma. All the same, when Spall woke up the next morning, it was not the memory of supper that put a smile on his face.

“I'm glad you're awake,” said Emma. “The baby needs changing.”
